Markov Decision Process

Making sequential decisions under uncertainty

Deterministic vs. Stochastic

f(n) = g(n) + h(n)
 
Deterministic: The outcome of an action is guaranteed. If the agent chooses to move right, it always moves right — no surprises. The environment is fully predictable, so we can plan an optimal path by reasoning about exact costs g(n) and heuristic estimates h(n). A* always finds the shortest path in a deterministic environment. Stochastic: The outcome of an action is uncertain and governed by probabilities. If the agent chooses to move right, it succeeds 80% of the time — but 10% of the time it slips perpendicular upward, and 10% downward. Because of this uncertainty, a fixed path plan can fail. We need a policy — a rule for every state — that works well on average across all possible outcomes. This is the setting that Markov Decision Processes are designed for.

Definitions

A sequential decision problem for a fully observable, stochastic environment with a Markovian transition model and additive rewards is called a Markov Decision Process.

\( S \) The 11 navigable squares on our grid. Where the agent is at time \( t \).
\( A(s) \) Up, Down, Left, Right — the set of legal moves from state \( s \).
\( P(s' \mid s, a) \) The physics of the slippery floor — probability of landing in \( s' \) when taking action \( a \) from \( s \).
\( R(s) \) The feedback the agent receives at each state. e.g. \( {+1} \) at the goal, \( {-1} \) at the trap, \( {-0.04} \) at every other step.

What does a solution look like?

A policy — a rule for every state.
\( \pi : S \to A, \qquad \pi(s) = a \)
For every state the agent might visit, the policy prescribes an action.

How do we measure the quality of a policy?

Additive reward
\( U = R(s_0) + R(s_1) + R(s_2) + \cdots = \displaystyle\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} R(s_t) \)
Every future reward counts equally.
Discounted reward
\( U = R(s_0) + \gamma R(s_1) + \gamma^2 R(s_2) + \cdots = \displaystyle\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t R(s_t) \)
A reward \( t \) steps away is worth \( \gamma^t \) of its face value. A reward now is better than the same reward later.  \( \gamma \in [0,1] \) is the discount factor.

Expected Total Utility

The utility of following policy \( \pi \) from state \( s \) is the expected sum of discounted rewards:
\( U^\pi(s) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\!\left[\, \sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t\, R(s_t) \;\Big|\; \pi,\; s_0 = s \,\right] \)
The optimal policy is the one that maximises this from every state:
\( \pi^{*} \;=\; \arg\max_{\pi}\; U^\pi(s) \)

The Bellman Equation

The utility of a state equals its immediate reward plus the discounted utility of the next state.
\( U^\pi(s) = R(s) + \gamma\, \mathbb{E}\!\left[ U^\pi(s') \mid s,\, \pi(s) \right] \)
\( R(s) \) reward received now
\( \gamma \) discount factor
\( P(s' \mid s,\, \pi(s)) \) probability of reaching \( s' \)
\( U^\pi(s') \) utility of the next state

How do we solve this recursive equation?

\( U^\pi(s) \) appears on both sides — we can't solve it directly.
Two algorithms find the solution:
Value Iteration
Start with arbitrary utilities. Repeatedly apply the Bellman update until values converge.
Policy Iteration
Start with an arbitrary policy. Alternate between evaluating the current policy and improving it — until the policy no longer changes.
