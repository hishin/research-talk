But that raises an interesting question: how did people make films before storyboarding became a standard practice?

Before storyboarding

Lack of shared vision · Difficulty communicating pacing and timing · Misinterpretations · Expensive to iterate

Before this animators worked with loose comic strip style story sketches. And each animator had to interpret how exactly to animate each scene. Similarly filmmakers relied heavily on screenplays and written shot lists, treating scenes like stage plays. As a result, there was no single visual reference that everyone on the production could share. This made it difficult to communicate crucial details such as pacing, and timing, and it left considerable room for misinterpretation. The problem was especially acute because iteration was expensive. In animation, every frame had to be drawn by hand. In live-action filmmaking, shooting and reshooting scenes required significant time and resources. Storyboarding emerged as a solution to these problems.

Storyboards work because they

  • represents story in semantic units
  • creates a shared reference
  • makes iteration cheap and easy

These principles apply in human-human collaboration, human-AI interaction and multi-agent coordination

So am I talking about storyboarding today? The reason is that the qualities that made storyboards so transformative extend far beyond filmmaking. Storyboards organize stories into semantic units, they create a shared reference that multiple people can point to and discuss, and they enable cheap and easy iteration. These same principles are essential whenever people need to collaborate on complex tasks. They matter in human-human collaboration, in human-AI interaction, and in multi-agent systems. My research explores this question through the lens of media authoring tools.
Compare the storyboard to a modern video editing interface. We live in a time when more media is being created than ever before. over 500 hours of video are uploaded to YouTube every minute...authoring and editing media remains an extremely difficult and tedious task. Many tools give us incredible precision, but often at the cost of ease of use.

Gulf of Execution

(Don Norman, 1986)

When creators think about making a lyric video, they have a high-level intention that can be simplified as: show the lyrics naturally within the scene. However the production process of converting music videos into lyric videos is challenging because users need to translate this high-level intention into many detached low-level operations, like segmenting text, adjusting the bounding boxes, aligning timestamps, picking colors and setting keyframes. The other part of the user's high-level intent is spatial. Finding an optimal placement has several components. We operationalize each of these into an energy function. Given the collection of frames spanned by a lyric phrase... First, we don't want to occlude the main subject in the video. So the first energy term computes the overlap between the text and the average foreground mask. Second, we want to place the lyrics near the salient region, where the viewer's attention is likely to be at. The second term tries to minimize the distance between the text and the center of mass of the average focus mask. Third, we want to place the text against background regions with high color contrast. So, we compute the color difference map between the text and the surrounding background pixels. And Finally, we want to place the text close to the previous phrase to have smooth eye gaze movement. So, we compute the distance between the text and the previous phrase's position. The optimal position of the text is the position that minimizes the weighted sum of the four energy terms.

Establish Shared References

between humans, between humans and AI and between agents

Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs

Many creative tasks are collaborative. To facilitate communication between collaborators, we need to establish shared references, whether it is between humans or between humans and AI. And one way to do this is through shared references. In a classic study, Clark and Wilkes-Gibbs asked pairs of participants to repeatedly communicate about abstract geometric shapes using only spoken language. Initially, participants relied on long, detailed descriptions to identify each shape, but as they repeated the process, they began to use simpler nicknames. This process illustrates the establishment of common ground. It's what makes communication efficient between collaborators.

One important way is through intermediate artifacts. Let me explain with an example of video editing. We interviewed expert video creators across different genres. Although their workflows varied, they all shared four core stages. They collected assets, like photos, footage and reference documents; they developed a storyline; they planned out the scenes on a storyboard; and they sequenced the scenes in the timeline editor. This was not a single pass linear process, but an iterative process. And each of these stages involved intermediate artifacts such as a canvas or a file system for collecting a set of assets, a text editor, a table for the storyboard, and the timeline. What's more interesting is that each of the intermediate artifacts have semantic structures that encode the relationship between its individual elements. So for example a canvas board or a file system of assets could encode a hierarchial relationship between individual assets; The text editor encodes a linear progression of narrative. The storyboard encodes alignment between the narration and the visual scene, and finally the timeline encodes the temporal synchrony between the clips.

User and AI collaborate through intermediate artifacts

Canvas View

First, users and AI collaborate through intermediate artifacts rather than communicating only through prompts and final outputs. On the canvas, users can collect assets, write notes, generate new notes, generate images, and generate videos. These artifacts are not just outputs; they become shared representations that both the user and the AI can build upon. They also provide context for generation. For example, when generating a note, the associated asset provides context. When generating a video, the assets and notes on the canvas become generation context.

Semantic structures enable synchronization

Because these artifacts encode semantic structure, they can also stay synchronized across different views. On the left, a user drags a note from the canvas into the narrative editor. Rather than simply copying text, the system transforms that note into content that fits its new location and takes surrounding content into account. On the right, the user moves from the narrative editor to the storyboard view. The narrative is automatically reorganized into storyboard elements. For example, a sentence such as “Show a close-up shot of a fortune cookie” is recognized as a visual instruction and placed in the visual column, while the accompanying explanation is placed in the voice-over column.

Open Research Questions

  • Supporting exploration across many parallel artifacts and agent-generated alternatives
  • Tracking the history of decisions made by both humans and agents
  • Designing productive friction, not just productivity
As AI systems become increasingly capable and agentic, I think this opens up several exciting research directions. First, as people begin working with multiple agents simultaneously, collaboration will no longer produce a single artifact or a single line of progress. Instead, users with the help of agentsmay be exploring many alternatives, hypotheses, and directions in parallel. How do we help people navigate, compare, and make sense of these branching spaces of work? Second, as both humans and agents contribute to a creative task, questions of history and provenance become increasingly important. How do we represent not only what decisions were made, but who made them, why they were made, and how they evolved over time? And finally, much of our work in HCI and AI has focused on reducing friction and increasing efficiency. But as systems become more capable, it is worth asking whether efficiency should always be the primary goal. What kinds of reflection, deliberation, or even productive friction help people remain engaged, creative, and in control of their work? Before I wrap up, I'd like to share a brief anecdote.

Who are we building for?

One of the privileges of working in an industry research lab is the opportunity to spend time with real users of the technologies we build. Just last week, I met two creators whose perspectives on AI seemed completely opposite. The first was a generative AI filmmaker who originally came from a traditional animation background. He told me that AI had fundamentally transformed his workflow. Previously, he specialized in character design. Today, he can direct and produce entire short films himself. He described AI as an amplifier of his creative capabilities. The second creator had a very different reaction. When we asked about agentic video editing features that could automatically edit footage from a prompt, he immediately pushed back. He told us, “I don’t like it. It takes the creative fun out of my job.” He described himself as an AI skeptic. At first glance, these creators appear to be on opposite sides of the debate. But I don’t think they are. What I heard from both of them was the same underlying message: people want tools that complement their abilities, not tools that do what they already do well and enjoy doing. As we continue building increasingly capable models, agents, and creative systems, I think this raises an important question: Who are we building for? We've put a enormous effort in building intelligent tools and models. But that's not enough. We need to build intelligently designed intelligent tools. Tools and models that are grounded in the needs and aspirations of the people who use them. And that's what's motivated my work. Thank you.

What do we mean by “effective”?

“General methods that leverage computation are ultimately the most effective.” — Richard Sutton, 2019

For creativity support tools, “effective” must go beyond task performance.

Creative Experience: How do we capture flow, agency, and enjoyment in human–AI collaboration?

Long-term Impact: How do AI systems shape creators and creative domains over time? Do they expand or narrow creative possibilities?

Sustainability: How do we account for environmental and societal costs beyond performance?

Only by developing robust ways to evaluate these dimensions can we design truly effective interfaces.

Sutton’s “bitter lesson” line is a foil: reward alone is too narrow for subjective media. Argue for mixed methods: creative experience, longitudinal effects on practice, and costs outside the lab. Tie back to the takeaways slide if someone asks how we would know a system succeeded.
