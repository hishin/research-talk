Three Little Pigs, 1933. Animated short film produced by Walt Disney.

"Who knows this movie?" [Pause for audience] Three Little Pigs, 1933 Disney. What is less well known is how this animated short was a breakthrough in animation technology. This film marked a major leap in production technique. It was the first cartoon to be fully storyboarded beat for beat — a technique credited to story man Webb Smith.
Here you can see the actual story sketches from the production — Scene I, II, III all planned out on paper. And on the right, this is what a story meeting looked like at the Disney studio. The whole team gathered around a board, discussing the structure of the film before a single frame was animated. The first story to be storyboarded. It was the first time an animated cartoon was fully planned out beat for beat.

"So what happened after Three Little Pigs?" [CLICK] In 1933, Walt Disney established the studio's first dedicated Story Department. [CLICK] By 1938, every major American animation studio had a dedicated story department. It spread incredibly fast. [CLICK] And it wasn't just animation. In 1939, William Cameron Menzies used storyboards to plan every single shot of Gone with the Wind. [CLICK] And then you have Hitchcock, who took it even further. In a 1969 interview with Roger Ebert he said: "Once the screenplay is finished, I'd just as soon not make the film at all. I have a strongly visual mind. I visualize a picture right down to the final cuts." So why was storyboarding so important? Three reasons: One — it allowed you to look at the entire structure of a film at once. Two — it acts as an intermediate artifact you can iterate on cheaply. Three — it expresses the most important semantics: the shot angle, the beat, the emotion, the character.

A storyboard…

  • organizes story into semantic units
  • creates a shared reference
  • enables easy iteration
So, why was Webb Smith's bulletin board so revolutionary? It did three things: First, it allowed creators to think in semantic units — "the wolf blows the house down" — rather than individual frames. Second, it created a shared reference point for the whole studio to communicate. And third, because of the thumbtacks, it became a flexible substrate for rapid iteration.
But a funny thing happened when we transitioned to digital video authoring. We lost the story board. Today, our most powerful timeline editors force us to manipulate timecodes and keyframes.

Gulf of Execution

(Don Norman, 1986)

When creators think about making a lyric video, they have a high-level intention that can be simplified as: show the lyrics on the screen naturally within the scene.However the production process of converting music videos into lyric videos is challenging because users need to translate this high-level intention into many detached low-level operations, like segmenting text, adjusting the bounding boxes, aligning timestamps, picking colors and setting keyframes. This is an example of what Don Norman called the Gulf of Execution. There's a big gap between the user's goal and what the system's interface allows them to do in order to achieve that goal.

Let's break down the user's intent. What it means to place lyrics naturally within the scene. First, there's the temporal element: lyrics should land in sync with the song, in chunks people can actually read. To achieve this, we have a pre-processing step to align words to the song and group them into phrases taking into account the beat of the song. We break lines for long lyric phrases. The other part of the user's high-level intent is spatial. Finding an optimal placement has several components. We operationalize each of these into an energy function. First, we don't want to occlude the main subject in the video. Given the collection of frames spanned by a lyric phrase, we compute the average foreground mask, and focus of attention mask. The first energy term computes the overlap between the text and the foreground mask. Second, we want to place the lyrics near the salient region, where the viewer's attention is likely to be at. The second term tries to minimize the distance between the text and the center of mass of the focus mask. Third, we want to place the text against background regions with high color contrast. And Finally, we want to place the text close to the previous phrase to maintain consistency.

Establish Common Ground

between humans, and between humans and AI through shared references.

That closes the lyric-video thread. Next theme is shared references: building common ground between people, and between people and models, so coordination does not depend on everyone staring at the same raw pixels.

People communicate by establishing common ground

When people coordinate, they rely on common ground: mutual knowledge that each side knows the other knows. A storyboard in a room full of artists is a classic example; everyone can point at the same panel. Live streaming revives that need at scale: thousands of viewers reacting to the same moving image in real time.

mirrorfly.com/blog/live-stream-chat

Chat is the default channel, but it is almost entirely text in a side column. That is fine for emotes and short reactions; it is a weak match when viewers want to refer to something specific on the video itself.

The problem with live stream interaction

sproutsocial.com/insights/twitch-marketing

  • Visual references are awkward in text chat.
  • Latency disrupts the communication.
  • Streamers must split attention between performing and reading chat.
Three friction points we heard in prior work and in our own studies: plain text makes deictic references clumsy; network delay breaks conversational rhythm; and performing while monitoring a firehose of chat is cognitively expensive. These set up why we need a richer shared reference than scrolling text alone.

Our insight was to use visual attributes as common ground. Instead of treating user's visual input as raw pixels, we decompose them into shared visual attributes, which become shared dimensions that streamers and viewers can use to make sense of the hundreds of inputs. For example, inputs can be decomposed into: position, shape, rotation, size, color. Position is where the mark sits; shape is the primitive viewers draw with; rotation and size often encode direction and magnitude; color can encode categories or votes. Intermediate artifacts encode semantic structures of its elements.

One important way is through intermediate artifacts. Let me explain with an example of video editing. We interviewed 9 expert video creators across different genres. Although their workflows varied, they all shared four core stages. They started with a collection of assets, like photos, footage and reference documents, then they developed a storyline; with these the editors planned out each scene by breaking down the story into narrative beats and matching visuals, much like the storyboard we discussed in the beginning. And the scenes were sequenced in the timeline editor to adjust pacing. This was not a single pass linear process, but an iterative process. {click} to reveal intermediate artifacts. And each of these stages involved intermediate artifacts such as a canvas board or a file system with a set of assets a document in a linear text editor, a grid or a table for the storyboard, and the sequences in the timeline editor. What's more interesting is that each of the intermediate artifacts have semantic structures that encode the relationship between its individual elements. So for example a canvas board or a file system of assets could encode a hierarchial relationship between individual assets; The text editor encodes a linear progression of narrative. The grid based storyboard encodes congruence between the narration and the visual scene, and finally the timeline encodes the temporal synchrony between the clips.

Effective interfaces should surface intermediate artifacts that encode semantic structures.

This is the key design principle: it's not enough to hide the complexity of AI pipelines behind a single chat box. Effective interfaces should make intermediate artifacts visible — and those artifacts should carry the semantic structure of the work. When a creator can see and manipulate a canvas of assets, a narrative document, a scene grid, and a timeline, they're working with representations that encode the meaning and relationships of their material. That structure is what makes AI assistance legible and controllable at each stage.

In our recent work VideoOrigami presented at CHI, we propose a design approach that leverages structured intermediate artifacts to support human AI co-creation. And we instantiate this approach in an agent-supported video editing interface which incorporated the 4 intermediate artifacts that are common to video workflows: a canvas, a document editor, a grid or storyboard and a timeline. Let me walk you through an example of a user using VideoOrigami to make an informational video about fortune cookies.

User and agent fluidly collaborate on intermediate artifacts.

Instead of the agent simply outputting the final timeline from a user prompt, in Video Origami, the user and the agent fluidly collaborate on intermediate artifacts. So here, the user collected some assets on a canvas, several source documents about fortune cookies. She invokes the agent to summarize one of the documents. The agent creates another intermediate artifact, a note with a summary of the article and links it with the orignal asset.

User and agent fluidly collaborate on intermediate artifacts.

Next, when the user moves into the narrative editor she can collaborate with the AI on the script. As she works on the script, the agent tracks provenance, linking each part of the narrative back to the source assets it draws from. So, AI is helping to synchronize across different intermediate artifacts.

User and agent fluidly collaborate on intermediate artifacts.

The narrative editor can be transformed into a grid — the storyboard. The agent suggests how to break the script into initial scenes and pair each one with appropriate visuals. Becuase intermediate artifacts are surfaces as first-class citizens, it is easy to go back and forth between the asset view and the storyboard view.

User and agent fluidly collaborate on intermediate artifacts.

When the user moves on to work on the timeline, each portion of the timeline corresponds directly to a cell in the scene plan. As the user selects a segment on the timeline, the corresponding cell in the scene plan is highlighted, and vice versa.

Takeaways

  1. 1. Support semantics — translate high-level creative intent into algorithms, bridging the gap between how people think and how tools operate.
  2. 2. Support common ground — enable shared references between humans, and between humans and AI, so collaboration stays grounded in what everyone can see.
  3. 3. Support iteration — creative work is never one-shot. Build tools that make the cycle of exploring, evaluating, and revising intuitive and fun.

What do we mean by “effective”?

“General methods that leverage computation are ultimately the most effective.” — Richard Sutton, 2019

For creativity support tools, “effective” must go beyond task performance.

Creative Experience: How do we capture flow, agency, and enjoyment in human–AI collaboration?

Long-term Impact: How do AI systems shape creators and creative domains over time? Do they expand or narrow creative possibilities?

Sustainability: How do we account for environmental and societal costs beyond performance?

Only by developing robust ways to evaluate these dimensions can we design truly effective interfaces.

Sutton’s “bitter lesson” line is a foil: reward alone is too narrow for subjective media. Argue for mixed methods: creative experience, longitudinal effects on practice, and costs outside the lab. Tie back to the takeaways slide if someone asks how we would know a system succeeded.
